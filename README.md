# Campus Waste Reduction Sustainability Proposal

## Overview

This project is an undergraduate engineering elective proposal focused on reducing the percentage of campus waste sent to landfill through sustainable material substitution.

The proposal responds to a University of Victoria Facilities Management request for ideas related to waste reduction and sustainability. It explores how replacing selected campus materials with biodegradable or recyclable alternatives could support landfill diversion, reduce environmental impact, and contribute to UVic’s broader sustainability goals.

The project combines environmental research, sustainability planning, material comparison, and proposal writing to present a practical campus-focused waste reduction strategy.

## Project Objective

The main objective of this project is to explore whether changing commonly used campus materials can help reduce the amount of waste sent to landfill.

The project focuses on:

* Reducing landfill waste
* Supporting UVic waste diversion goals
* Comparing reusable, biodegradable, and recyclable materials
* Investigating biodegradable plastic as a possible alternative
* Considering recycling plastic and material cost trends
* Understanding landfill and pollution impacts
* Proposing research and student survey methods
* Supporting campus sustainability decision-making

## Background

Waste management is a major sustainability issue for universities and urban communities. The project identifies landfill waste as an environmental concern because improper waste management can contribute to soil contamination, water contamination, air pollution, climate impacts, and harm to animal and marine life.

The proposal is connected to UVic’s waste reduction goals, including increasing the waste diversion rate, reducing total waste per campus user, and improving waste reduction education and engagement. UVic’s Sustainability Action Plan included goals such as increasing waste diversion to 82% and reducing waste production relative to a 2010 baseline.

## Problem Statement

Many campus activities generate disposable or reusable material waste, especially in food-related services and daily student life.

If materials are not properly reused, recycled, or composted, they may be sent to landfill and contribute to long-term environmental damage.

This project asks:

**Can campus landfill waste be reduced by substituting selected materials with more sustainable alternatives such as biodegradable materials and recyclable plastics?**

## Proposed Sustainability Strategy

The proposal focuses on material substitution as a practical waste reduction strategy.

The main material strategies include:

### 1. Biodegradable Materials

Biodegradable materials can break down through the action of microorganisms into natural substances such as water, carbon dioxide, and biomass under suitable environmental conditions.

The project discusses biodegradable plastics as a potential replacement for selected campus materials, especially where composting and proper collection systems are available.

### 2. Recycling Plastic

The project also considers recycling plastic as part of a waste reduction strategy.

Plastic waste is a major environmental issue in Canada, and recycling can help divert material from landfill. However, the proposal also recognises that the effectiveness of recycled plastic depends on cost, demand, production conditions, and the relationship between virgin plastic and recycled plastic markets.

### 3. Campus Engagement

The project proposes student surveys as a way to understand how students approach recycling and waste reduction on campus.

This is important because waste reduction is not only a material problem. It also depends on user behaviour, awareness, education, and accessibility of waste sorting systems.

## Research Objectives

The proposal identifies two major research components:

### Research Review

The research review aims to investigate:

* Landfill issues
* Waste diversion problems
* Pollution impacts
* Biodegradable material options
* Recycling strategies
* Existing successful waste reduction projects
* UVic sustainability initiatives

### Student Surveys

The student survey component aims to understand:

* Student recycling behaviours
* Awareness of campus waste systems
* International perspectives on recycling
* Barriers to proper waste sorting
* Practical challenges in reducing landfill waste

Together, these research methods are intended to support a more evidence-based campus waste reduction recommendation.

## Connection to UVic Sustainability Goals

The proposal aligns with UVic’s sustainability priorities by supporting:

* Higher waste diversion rates
* Lower total waste per campus user
* Better student engagement in waste reduction
* More sustainable campus operations
* Improved material choices in daily campus life

The project also connects to UVic’s Recyclepedia and campus waste reduction resources as part of a broader sustainability engagement strategy.

## Key Findings and Insights

The project highlights several important ideas:

* Solid waste remains a major environmental issue in Canada.
* Landfill disposal contributes to environmental risks such as soil, water, air, and climate contamination.
* Biodegradable materials may help reduce landfill waste when supported by proper disposal and composting systems.
* Recycling plastic can support waste diversion, but market and cost conditions affect its practical value.
* Campus waste reduction requires both material substitution and behaviour change.
* Student engagement is important because users directly affect sorting, recycling, and disposal outcomes.
* Universities can act as testing grounds for practical sustainability solutions.

## Relevance to Engineering and Applied Problem Solving

This project demonstrates how engineering-style thinking can be applied to sustainability challenges.

The project shows the ability to:

* Identify a real environmental problem
* Connect the problem to institutional goals
* Compare possible material-based solutions
* Consider technical and behavioural factors
* Develop research objectives

Although this project does not include a built prototype, it demonstrates early-stage engineering proposal development and sustainability-focused problem framing.

## Limitations

This project is a proposal rather than an implemented waste reduction program.

Its limitations include:

* No pilot implementation
* No direct measurement of campus waste reduction
* Limited quantitative cost-benefit analysis
* No lifecycle assessment of material alternatives
* No detailed composting infrastructure analysis
* No completed student survey dataset
* Limited technical comparison between biodegradable and recyclable materials
* Some recommendations would require further feasibility testing

## Future Improvements

This project could be strengthened by:

* Conducting student surveys and analysing responses
* Estimating current campus landfill waste by material type
* Comparing lifecycle impacts of biodegradable and recyclable materials
* Adding cost estimates for material substitution
* Evaluating composting and recycling infrastructure requirements
* Interviewing UVic Facilities Management staff
* Creating an implementation roadmap
* Developing a pilot program for one campus location
* Measuring before-and-after waste diversion outcomes
* Building a dashboard to track campus waste reduction progress

## Conclusion

This project presents a sustainability proposal for reducing campus landfill waste through more responsible material choices and improved waste reduction engagement.

By examining biodegradable materials, recycling strategies, landfill impacts, and UVic sustainability goals, the proposal demonstrates how material substitution can be used as part of a broader campus waste reduction strategy.

The project highlights the importance of combining environmental research, user behaviour, institutional goals, and practical implementation planning when addressing sustainability challenges.
